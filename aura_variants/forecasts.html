§ 1 · What is this feature?

Public predictions, public grades. Every quarter.

The Calibrated Forecast Board is the AURA Lab's signature research-accountability instrument. Every quarter we publish a small set of falsifiable claims about where software-engineering AI is going. Each claim has a precise resolution rule, an explicit time horizon, and our confidence as a number between 0 and 100%.

When the horizon arrives we score the prediction. We use the Brier score — a standard, unambiguous proper-scoring rule for probabilistic forecasts. Our running mean Brier score sits at the top of the page so anyone can see how calibrated the lab actually is. There is no hiding place.

The point is not to be right. The point is to know how often we are.

Most labs publish only their successes. A few publish their failures. None of them, to our knowledge, commit publicly to a quantitative track record on predictions about their own field. This board is our attempt to do that.

Below: the methodology (§2), the scoring rule with worked examples (§3), the active and settled forecasts (§4), how the community participates (§5), and the FAQ that catches the questions we get most often (§6).

§ 2 · Why we built it

A field's calibration is the single most undervalued metric in research culture.

If a research community routinely claims things will happen by some date and they don't — and the community moves on — that community has a calibration problem. It will make poor allocations of student time, of grant money, of strategic attention. It will believe its own press.

The lab's third research thread, Reliable, argues that honest evaluation is undervalued in code AI. This board extends that argument to the lab itself. If we believe better calibration would improve science, the first place to demonstrate it is on our own forecasts.

The right metric for a research lab is the one its publishers would still publish if it embarrassed them.
Concept

Forecast

A precise, falsifiable claim about the world by a specific date — with the lab's stated probability and an unambiguous resolution rule.

Concept

Confidence

Our stated probability the forecast resolves true. Updated over time as evidence arrives; the sparkline on each card shows the drift.

Concept

Brier score

The proper scoring rule we use to grade ourselves. Lower is better. Mathematically: average of (p − o)² across all settled forecasts.

B = (1/N) · Σ (pi − oi)²
§ 3 · How the score works

Brier score, demystified — with three worked examples.

The Brier score punishes confidence in the wrong direction and rewards calibrated humility. The lower your average, the better you are at saying "70%" only when you actually mean 70%.

Three worked examples

A

You say 75 %, it happens.

You were confident in the right direction, but not all the way. Brier rewards you, but reminds you that the world said "yes" while you only said "0.75."

B = (0.75 − 1)² = 0.0625 — a strong score.
B

You say 60 %, it doesn't happen.

You leaned toward yes, the world said no. You eat the cost in proportion to how confident you were on the wrong side.

B = (0.60 − 0)² = 0.36 — a bad score that should make you re-examine your priors.
C

You say 50 %, anything happens.

You expressed no view. The Brier scorer charges you 0.25 either way. Saying 50 % is the floor of intellectual honesty; you can do better by actually predicting.

B = (0.5 − 0)² or (0.5 − 1)² = 0.25 either way.
§ 4 · The protocol

Six rules that keep this honest.

Calibration only works if the rules are tamper-resistant. We commit publicly to these six.

01

Pre-registration

Every forecast is published before the resolution horizon. Timestamp is visible on the card.

02

No post-hoc edits

Wording, threshold, and resolution rule are frozen at publication. The confidence value can drift as evidence arrives, but every drift is logged.

03

Unambiguous resolution rule

Each card has a one-sentence "Resolution:" line. If two competent referees would disagree about the outcome, we rewrite the rule.

04

Settle on time

When the horizon date arrives, we publish the outcome within 30 days. No silent expirations.

05

Community can challenge

Anyone can submit a counter-claim or a new forecast. Submissions are tagged and merged into the public posterior.

06

Honest failure

Refuted forecasts stay visible forever. The lab's Brier score includes them; the failures are the score.

§ 6 · FAQ

Questions we get most often.

Isn't this just a glorified blog of predictions?

No. A blog of predictions is unscored. The forecast board commits to a proper scoring rule (Brier), publishes the lab's running mean score on the homepage, and includes every refuted forecast in the score forever.

Refuted forecasts can't be quietly deleted; they're load-bearing for the calibration we display.

Why Brier and not log loss?

Both are proper. Brier is bounded (in [0,1]) and easier to communicate; log loss penalises 0% predictions with infinite cost, which is hard to recover from a single confident wrong answer. We chose Brier as the public-facing score and report log loss in the methodology appendix for the curious.

Couldn't you game this by only predicting safe outcomes?

You could. We try to keep ourselves honest by mixing easy and hard forecasts, and by publishing the shape of the calibration plot — not just the mean Brier. A coward's calibration concentrates around 50 %; the calibration plot reveals it instantly.

What happens when a forecast becomes ambiguous?

If two independent referees can't agree on the outcome, we publish the forecast as mixed and assign it the worst-case Brier penalty. This is a deliberately punitive convention. It pushes us to write better resolution rules in the first place.

How do community submissions affect the lab's score?

Community-submitted forecasts are scored separately and do not count toward the lab's Brier mean. We do publish a community calibration chart alongside ours, which has been a fun source of internal debate when community priors diverge sharply from the lab's.

Can I cite the board?

Yes. Cite as: AURA Lab. Calibrated Forecasts. 2026. https://aura-se-lab.github.io/forecasts. A versioned snapshot of the board is archived quarterly so you can cite a specific resolution.
