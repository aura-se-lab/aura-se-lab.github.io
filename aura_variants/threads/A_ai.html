Thread A · AIResource-efficient foundation models

Smaller models, same proof. A research programme for code intelligence that fits on a developer's laptop.

A 200-billion-parameter model that runs in a datacentre is not the same product as a 200-million-parameter model that runs on a M-series MacBook. The lab studies the engineering and the science of getting the second one to do the first one's job — measurably, and without hiding the cost.

§ 1 · Why this matters

Cost is a property of the model, not an inconvenience around it.

A model that achieves 84 % accuracy on a leaderboard and takes 38 kilowatt-hours per evaluation is, in deployment, a worse model than one that achieves 78 % at 1 kilowatt-hour. The first cannot be run in a developer's IDE, cannot be audited by a small university lab, cannot be priced into a budget. It is a research curiosity. The second is software.

This is the substantive argument for the AURA Lab's first research thread. Across the field, headline numbers from very large code models are reported with quiet asterisks attached to the energy, the latency, the carbon footprint, and the deployment cost. Practitioners and reviewers alike then make decisions as if the cost were zero. It is not.

A model whose energy budget is not declared cannot be compared.

We treat efficiency as an evaluation dimension equal in standing to accuracy. We report parameters, latency, peak memory, and energy alongside every quality metric — for our own models and for the baselines we run. We publish the measurement scripts. We accept submissions to our benchmarks that disagree with our numbers.

The downstream effect is concrete: developers in the IDE who can run our distilled code-summarisation model locally, on CPU, in under 800 ms; reviewers who can rerun our experiments on a single workstation; institutions that can adopt these systems without procuring a datacentre.

The three failure modes we study
  • Latency that excludes interactive use
    If autocomplete blocks for 2 s, the developer turned it off three weeks ago.
  • Memory that excludes on-device deployment
    If the model needs 80 GB of VRAM, it cannot be the privacy story you wanted.
  • Energy that excludes academic replication
    If reproducing a result costs $40 K, only six labs in the world will check it.
§ 2 · What is hard

Three honest answers about why small models for code are not solved.

2.1 · Distillation drift

Student models inherit shortcuts, not understanding.

A 1.1 B student trained to imitate a 70 B teacher on summarisation easily reproduces the surface fluency and loses the chain-of-thought. Standard distillation does not transfer reasoning — only output tokens. The remedy is task-aware distillation that supervises intermediate behaviours, which requires inventing the right intermediates.

2.2 · Quantisation cliffs

4-bit is fine, until it suddenly is not.

Aggregate accuracy holds steady under 4-bit quantisation, then collapses on specific input distributions (long context, rare identifiers, low-resource languages). A benchmark average hides the cliff. ICSME 2025 catalogues where the cliffs live, by language and by task.

2.3 · PEFT & the tuning trap

LoRA gives you eight knobs and a moral hazard.

Parameter-efficient fine-tuning lowers compute per training run, which encourages a larger sweep. The aggregate cost can exceed full fine-tuning. We argue, in the TOSEM 2025 survey, that the comparison should be measured in total energy to first usable model, not per-step cost.

§ 3 · How we ensure it

Method — measure twice, distil once, publish everything.

  • Joint metric pact
    Every paper reports {parameters, latency, memory, energy, accuracy}. No partial reports.
  • Honest baselines
    We re-run baselines under the same protocol — never copy numbers from the original paper.
  • One-command reproduce
    Each artefact ships with ./reproduce.sh that rebuilds the headline figure end-to-end, including all efficiency numbers.
  • On-device benchmarking
    When a paper claims interactive latency, we measure on a developer machine, not an A100 cluster.
distill.py · excerpt 
# Task-aware distillation: supervise on intermediate
# representations, not just final tokens.
from aura import Teacher, Student, EnergyMeter

teacher = Teacher.load("codellama-13b")
student = Student(arch="distilbert-220m")
meter   = EnergyMeter("rapl")         # on-device joule counts

with meter.track("distillation") as energy:
    for batch in code_corpus(repos="top-1k"):
        t_logits, t_attn = teacher(batch, return_attn=True)
        s_logits, s_attn = student(batch, return_attn=True)
        loss = task_loss(s_logits, batch.label) \
             + 0.5 * attn_kl(s_attn, t_attn)   # transfer reasoning
        loss.backward(); student.step()

# Required honesty: report energy alongside accuracy.
print(f"BLEU = {student.eval(test).bleu:.2f}   "
      f"E = {energy.kwh:.2f} kWh   "
      f"params = {student.n_params/1e6:.0f} M")

§ 5 · For practitioners

What you can use today.

  • aura-lab/forge-25-summarization
    Distilled 220 M code-summarisation model. Docker image, < 1 s inference on CPU, MIT-licensed.
  • BRACE benchmark suite
    Run accuracy + energy + latency on your model with one command. Submit your result to the public leaderboard.
  • PEFT decision matrix
    Table from the TOSEM 2025 survey: pick LoRA / QLoRA / adapters / IA³ given your data, compute, and target model. Decision tree as a one-pager.
  • Energy meter
    RAPL + nvidia-smi wrapper. Drop it into any training loop and get a per-experiment kWh figure. Used in all our papers.
§ 6 · For researchers

Open problems.

  • Quantisation cliffs
    For each task × language × bitwidth, where exactly does accuracy collapse? We have a partial atlas; we want collaborators to fill it in.
  • Task-aware distillation
    Beyond logit distillation: what intermediates transfer? Attention? Activations? Surrogate plans?
  • Energy as a constraint
    Pareto-optimal training under a hard kWh budget — formal treatment, plus practical optimisers.
  • Honest leaderboards
    Multi-axis leaderboards (accuracy + energy + cost) with audited submissions. We host one; we'd like more groups to host theirs.
