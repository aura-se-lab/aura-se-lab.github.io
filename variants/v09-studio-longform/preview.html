The AURA Lab — AI for Understandable and Responsible Automation in Software Engineering.

A research practice for AI-driven software engineering that is efficient, explainable, and responsibly built.

William & Mary, Department of Computer Science Founded Fall 2024 Director, Dr. Antonio Mastropaolo
I. Statement

The AURA Lab, founded in Fall 2024 at William & Mary, advances research at the intersection of efficiency, automation, and explainability in software engineering. Our mission is to design innovative and responsible AI-driven solutions that improve developer productivity while addressing key challenges of transparency and resource efficiency.

Through rigorous methods, open collaboration, and a commitment to impactful work, the AURA Lab contributes practical advances in AI-supported software engineering for academia, industry, and society. The work is deliberately slow where it must be slow — careful empirical evaluation, reproducible benchmarks, instrumented studies — and fast where the field is fast — new models, new tooling, new questions every quarter.

We treat automation as a craft. Every claim has a workload behind it; every artifact has a release. We publish what we build, and we build what we can explain.

II. Research Avenues
I.

Explainability & Interpretability

Methods that reveal how code models reason — rationales, traces, executable tests — so that developers can inspect, trust, and debug automation rather than accept it as a black box. The lab studies what makes a model's behavior legible to a working engineer.

II.

Efficiency & Resource-Aware Automation

Quantization, distillation, and parameter-efficient fine-tuning to deliver low-latency, memory-light, cost-aware automation. Claims are measured on real workloads with reproducible metrics — not on synthetic micro-benchmarks divorced from the developer's machine.

III.

Multi-Agent LLMs for Software Engineering

Role-aligned agents that plan, code, test, and review — coordinated to produce coherent, end-to-end developer assistance. The work focuses on protocols of cooperation between agents and the human engineer they ultimately serve.

IV.

Neurosymbolic AI for Software Engineering

Hybrid pipelines pairing large language models with program analysis and explicit constraints, producing fixes, tests, and designs that are interpretable and checkable. We treat the symbolic layer as a guarantor of correctness, not as a relic.

V.

Task-Aware Code Automation

From issues, diffs, and traces to concise summaries, TODOs, and plans — models that reduce developer toil with actionable, repository-aware guidance. A supporting direction that informs every other strand of the lab's work.

III. Selected Publications

  1. TOSEM · 2024

    From Triumph to Uncertainty: The Journey of Software Engineering in the AI Era.

    Mastropaolo, A.; Escobar-Velásquez, C.; Linares-Vásquez, M.

    A reflective survey of how AI-assisted tooling has reshaped — and unsettled — the discipline of software engineering. Published in ACM Transactions on Software Engineering and Methodology.

  2. ICPC · 2025

    Optimizing Datasets for Code Summarization: Is Code-Comment Coherence Enough?

    Vitale, A.; Mastropaolo, A.; Oliveto, R.; Di Penta, M.; Scalabrino, S.

    Examines whether code-comment coherence is a sufficient training signal for summarization, and what dataset choices actually move the needle.

  3. FORGE · 2025

    Resource-Efficient & Effective Code Summarization.

    Afrin, S.; Call, J.; Nguyen, K.-N.; Chaparro, O.; Mastropaolo, A.

    A pragmatic look at how compact, well-trained models can match larger systems on code summarization while remaining cheap to run.

  4. ICPC ERA · 2025

    Toward Neurosymbolic Program Comprehension.

    Velasco, A.; Garryyeva, A.; Palacio, D. N.; Mastropaolo, A.; Poshyvanyk, D.

    An early-research-achievement vision for pairing neural code models with symbolic reasoning over program structure.

  5. ICSME · 2025

    Is Quantization a Deal-breaker? Empirical Insights from Large Code Models.

    Afrin, S.; Xu, B.; Mastropaolo, A.

    Empirical evidence on when aggressive quantization preserves — and when it breaks — the behavior of large code models in realistic SE tasks.

  6. FSE Ideas · 2025

    A Path Less Traveled: Reimagining Software Engineering Automation via a Neurosymbolic Paradigm.

    Mastropaolo, A.; Poshyvanyk, D.

    A position paper sketching a neurosymbolic agenda for SE automation grounded in interpretability and verifiable behavior.

IV. People

The lab is directed by Antonio Mastropaolo, Assistant Professor of Computer Science at William & Mary. He earned his Ph.D. at Università della Svizzera Italiana in 2024, and previously completed his M.Sc. and B.Sc. at the University of Molise. The director's research line — efficient, explainable, and responsible automation — sets the direction the lab takes up day to day.

In September 2024, Saima Afrin joined as the lab's first doctoral student; her work centers on resource-efficient code automation and the empirical study of quantization for large code models. In January 2025, Aya Garryyeva and Joseph Call joined the lab — Aya contributes to neurosymbolic program comprehension, and Joseph to summarization and code-aware language modeling. Khai-Nguyen Nguyen, Alvi, Ben, and Chris round out the current cohort of students, working across the lab's four primary research avenues.

The lab's work is sustained by a circle of frequent collaborators: Denys Poshyvanyk (William & Mary), David Nader Palacio, Alejandro Velasco, Oscar Chaparro, and Bowen Xu. These collaborations shape the lab's emphasis on rigorous empirical method and on bridging neural and symbolic approaches to software engineering automation.
